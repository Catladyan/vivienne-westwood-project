The Life of Vivian Westwood

The Very Beginning

Vivienne Westwood, born Vivienne Isabel Swire in 1941, hailed from Tintwistle, Cheshire. Her father worked in an aircraft factory, and the family later moved to Harrow, Greater London. Westwood briefly attended a jewellery and silversmith course at Harrow Art School but left due to uncertainties about her future in the art world. She then worked in a factory and pursued teacher training, eventually becoming a primary-school teacher. During this time, she began creating jewellery and selling it at Portobello Road. In 1962, she married Derek Westwood, whom she met while he was apprenticing at a Hoover factory. They had a son named Benjamin in 1963.

Preschool Teacher to 430 Kings Road

Vivienne Westwood's journey in fashion began with designing Teddy Boy clothes for Malcolm McLaren. In 1971, they opened Let it Rock, a small boutique on Kings Road, Chelsea, London. The store evolved, focusing on biker clothing, zips, and leather, leading to its re-branding as Too Fast to Live, Too Young to Die. Their provocative t-shirts sparked controversy and legal issues, prompting further re-branding to Sex in 1974, where they introduced rubberwear for the office. The shop morphed into Seditionaries in response to the rise of punk, embodying a DIY aesthetic. However, disillusionment followed the mainstream adoption of punk. In 1980, the shop became Worlds End, which remains its name today. Despite minor renovations, the original interior, designed by Vivienne and Malcolm, persists, reflecting their enduring legacy in fashion.

In 1984, Vivienne Westwood closed her London shop and moved to Italy. She showcased her 'Hypnos' collection in Tokyo in 1984 alongside other top designers. She opened a new boutique in London in 1988. The iconic orb logo was introduced in 1986. The 'Pirate' Collection in 1981, co-designed with Malcolm McLaren, marked their first collaborative show. Westwood's designs blend traditional cuts with innovative techniques.

Pagan

1988-1993

introduced in 1982, was a bold departure from conventional fashion. Inspired by pagan and tribal influences, the collection featured avant-garde designs with elements such as asymmetrical cuts, draped fabrics, and bold patterns. It challenged traditional notions of beauty and embraced a more primal, raw aesthetic. The Pagan collection represented Westwood's ongoing exploration of unconventional themes and her commitment to pushing the boundaries of fashion.

Anglomania

1993-1999

introduced in the early 1990s, Anglomania celebrates British heritage and culture while incorporating Westwood's signature avant-garde style. The collection often features elements such as tartan prints, corsetry, draping, and exaggerated silhouettes. Anglomania reflects Westwood's penchant for challenging conventional fashion norms and her commitment to sustainability and ethical fashion practices. The collection has gained recognition for its bold, edgy designs that fuse tradition with modernity, making it a cornerstone of British fashion.

Queer Culture

Westwood's impact on the queer community stems from her celebration of androgyny and non-conformity in fashion. Through her boundary-pushing designs and fearless self-expression, she empowered individuals to embrace their unique identities and challenge societal norms, making her an icon of freedom and self-acceptance within the queer community.

Westwood's Two Cowboys t-shirt, featuring two pantsless cowboys interacting, challenged Societal acceptance and sparked conversations about LGBTQ+ representation in fashion. Activest

Westwood is renowned for her outspoken activism, particularly in environmental and social justice causes. Through her fashion shows, campaigns, and public statements, she has consistently used her platform to advocate for climate action, human rights, and political reform. Westwood's bold stance on issues has inspired many within and beyond the fashion industry to engage in activism and promote positive change.

The Forever Iconic Dame's Badass Moments

TANK

Westwood made headlines in 2014 when she drove a tank to then-Prime Minister David Cameron's residence as part of a protest against fracking.

DAME

Westwood was appointed Dame Commander of the Order of the British Empire in 2006 for her services to fashion. However, she preformed a knickerless flash at Buckingham Palace after her acceptance.

CAGE

In 2016, Westwood made a statement by placing herself in a cage outside the Old Bailey courthouse in London to protest the extradition of Julian Assange.
